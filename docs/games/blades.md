# Blades in the Dark — How to Play

Blades is a heist game. You plan less, improvise more, and use flashbacks to reveal what your character prepared before the score started. The game is built around: free play → engagement roll → the score → downtime. Repeat.

## The Character Sheet

Inkandbone auto-builds your scoundrel: 4 points distributed across 12 action ratings (0-2 each), Stress 0, Trauma 0, Coin 2, Load 3, all XP tracks at 0. Create one through **⚙ Manage → Characters → + New Character**.

**Action ratings** are what you roll. 12 of them: Hunt, Study, Survey, Tinker, Finesse, Prowl, Skirmish, Wreck, Attune, Command, Consort, Sway. Each dot is one die.

**Stress** is your most-tracked resource. Spend it to push yourself, resist harm, or use special abilities. Fill 8 boxes and you take a Trauma. 4 Traumas and the character is done.

**Trauma** is permanent scarring. It changes how you play. When you fill that 9th Stress box, mark a Trauma instead.

**Coin** is money. **Stash** is saved wealth (converts to XP). **Load** determines how much gear you can carry.

**Playbook** is your archetype: Cutter, Hound, Leech, Lurk, Slide, Spider, Whisper. Each has unique special abilities.

## How the GM Runs Blades

The AI establishes **Position** (Desperate, Risky, Controlled) and **Effect** (Limited, Standard, Great) before every roll. Always ask: "What's my position and effect here?" before committing.

The AI tracks **Heat** (law enforcement attention), **Faction standings** (who likes you, who wants you dead), and **Clocks** (progress toward goals or disasters).

The AI does not over-plan for you. It reacts. You drive the score with flashbacks — declare a preparation after the fact and the AI assigns a Stress cost based on how plausible it is.

## Playing

Open the browser. Get your campaign, character, and session active. The conversation is the score:

> *I slip through the alley shadows toward the Ironhook's loading dock. The guard patrol passes every four minutes. I've got a two-minute window. I Finesse the lock on the side gate.*

The AI sets Position and Effect. You roll your Finesse rating in d6s. Best die tells the story: 1-3 is failure, 4-5 is partial success (you pay a price), 6 is full success. Multiple 6s is a critical.

When something goes wrong, you can **Resist** — spend Stress to reduce the consequence. The AI tells you how much Stress it costs based on the severity.

When you need something you didn't plan for, call a **Flashback**. *"I bribed that guard last week. Flashback."* The AI sets a Stress cost. Pay it and the preparation is established.

## The Score Structure

1. **Free Play** — gather information, acquire assets, make rolls to set up the score
2. **Engagement Roll** — determines how deep in trouble you start. A high roll puts you ahead. A low roll drops you into the action immediately
3. **The Score** — one big obstacle after another. Use flashbacks. Push yourself. Resist consequences
4. **Downtime** — between scores: recover Stress, reduce Heat, pursue Vice, advance your Playbook and Crew

## Key Rules

- **Push Yourself:** Spend 2 Stress to add +1d to a roll
- **Devil's Bargain:** The AI offers bonus dice in exchange for a complication. Take it. It makes the story better
- **Resist:** Spend Stress to reduce or avoid a consequence. The AI names the cost — usually 2-6 Stress depending on severity
- **Flashback:** Declare a preparation retroactively. Cost: 0 Stress (minor preparation) to 3+ Stress (major retcon)
- **Clocks:** The AI tracks progress as pie-chart clocks. Ask "what clocks are ticking?"

## First Time? Run a Score

Don't over-prepare. Pick a target (a merchant lord, a rival gang's stash, a locked room in a government building). Make an Engagement roll. Let the AI tell you where the score starts — already in motion, not at the planning table. Use flashbacks to fill in what you prepared.

Between scores, the AI runs downtime. Recover Stress. Pick a Vice to indulge. Advance one XP track that matches how you played.
